Air Quality vs. Economic Activity in the U.S.

Overall Introduction

As industrialization and urbanization accelerates across the world, air pollution has become a cause of concern for many. Nations like India, Bangladesh, and Chad face the worst air quality indexes, and their citizens face the dire consequences on a daily basis. However, this air pollution is often the result of massive economic turnover — new industries bringing jobs and wealth to otherwise low-income populations. To explore this tradeoff in the United States, a key question arises: Is there a tradeoff between economic activity and air quality?

Our initial research revealed key insights that shaped our thesis. A study from the National Library of Medicine discusses the medical effects of pollution while identifying major contributors, primarily various industries including utilities (gas, electric, water), transportation, and agriculture. A separate paper from Harvard specifically focuses on coal-fired power plants and their outsized contribution to worsening air quality across the country.

To learn more about the relationship between industrial activity and air quality, visit the National Library of Medicine or read Harvard's research on industrial air pollution.

This analysis is limited to state-level aggregates from the EIA, which may obscure finer-grained patterns at the county or city level. Additionally, AQI is influenced by geographic and meteorological factors beyond industrial activity alone. Future work could incorporate population density, GDP by sector, or longitudinal health outcome data to build a more complete picture of how economic activity shapes the air Americans breathe.
